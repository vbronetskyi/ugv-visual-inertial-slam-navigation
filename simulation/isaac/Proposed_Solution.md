# Chapter 4: proposed solution

This chapter describes the visual-inertial teach-and-repeat (T&R) stack
i built for an outdoor RGB-D + IMU UGV.  The idea is simple: drive the
route once (the *teach* run), record everything you'll need, then
re-drive it autonomously (the *repeat* run) and stay on track even when
VIO drifts, the scene changes, or someone drops obstacles on the path

The whole thing is a ROS 2 stack glued together: Isaac Sim provides
sensors, ORB-SLAM3 RGB-D-Inertial does the VIO, a custom node fuses
that with wheel encoders + periodic anchor corrections from re-matched
teach landmarks, Nav2 plans on a depth-derived map of the teach run, a
custom dispatcher handles route-level detours, and pure-pursuit drives
the wheels

## 4.1 design goals and constraints

Target platform is a Clearpath Husky A200 with a single Intel RealSense
D435i (RGB-D + IMU) and no GNSS.  Routes are 150-400 m round-trip
through moderately structured forest / park.  three goals fall out of
that:

- **no pre-built map**.  the only map is what the teach run itself
  produces - depth-derived occupancy, VIO trajectory, ORB landmark bag
- **localization that survives VIO drift**.  pure RGB-D-Inertial
  ORB-SLAM3 drifts 1-10 m over 400 m on texture-poor scenes; the repeat
  pipeline has to keep going without diverging
- **route-level obstacle avoidance**.  a tent or cone cluster on the
  teach line should trigger a real detour, not a tangential brush-past

The system was built and tested in NVIDIA Isaac Sim with a procedural
forest scene (~530 collision primitives - trees, shrubs, rocks,
buildings, props).  GT poses from the simulator are used only for
post-hoc evaluation - every control decision uses the fused SLAM pose

## 4.2 architecture

![pipeline architecture](system_architecture.png)

The diagram (above) lays out the five concurrent ROS 2 graphs at repeat
time.  in plain words:

- Isaac Sim publishes RGB, depth, IMU, `/odom` and a depth point cloud
- ORB-SLAM3 RGB-D-Inertial reads RGB + depth + IMU, emits a VIO pose
- `tf_wall_clock_relay_v55 --slam-encoder` fuses VIO + encoder + anchor
  corrections, publishes `map -> base_link` (4 fusion regimes)
- a depth-derived occupancy map from teach (`teach_map.pgm`) is the
  static layer of Nav2's global costmap; an obstacle layer learns
  props from the live depth cloud
- Nav2's `planner_server` (NavFn) plans on top, output to `/plan`
- `pure_pursuit_path_follower` reads `/plan`, writes `/cmd_vel` back
  into Isaac
- in parallel, `visual_landmark_matcher` (~2 Hz) re-matches the current
  frame against the teach `landmarks.pkl` and publishes
  `/anchor_correction` to the relay
- on top of that, `send_goals_hybrid` walks the teach WP list with WP
  projection + detour ring + look-ahead skip, and
  `turnaround_supervisor` drops obstacles when GT is within 10 m of
  the apex

## 4.3 teach phase

The teach run drives the robot once along a pre-planned corner-to-
corner trajectory and dumps four artefacts to disk:

- `vio_pose_dense.csv` - dense VIO + GT pose log at ~20 Hz, written by
  `vio_drift_monitor.py`.  at repeat time this is subsampled at 4 m
  spacing to get the teach waypoints
- `teach_map.pgm` / `teach_map.yaml` - depth-derived occupancy map at
  0.1 m resolution, built by `teach_run_depth_mapper.py` from every
  valid depth pixel
- `landmarks.pkl` - bag of ORB visual landmarks, recorded
  opportunistically: every time accumulated baseline goes past 2 m
  *and* the frame has >=30 keypoints with valid depth + enough local
  variance.  each landmark is *(camera pose, 2D + 3D keypoints, ORB
  descriptors, timestamp)*
- `traj_gt.csv` - ground-truth trajectory.  kept for evaluation only,
  never read at repeat time

Teach uses `tf_wall_clock_relay.py --use-gt` so the published TF is GT
rather than VIO; that way the recorded artefacts have no drift baked
in.  if VIO accumulates more than 10 m of drift vs GT, the run aborts
and retries

Routes are generated by `_common/scripts/generate_routes.py`: A* on an
inflated occupancy grid (clearance >=2 m + robot half-width 0.4 m),
Chaikin smoothing (2 iters), 0.8 m uniform resample, smooth 1.5 m
U-turn at the apex, parallel-offset return.  Smooth enough that VIO
does not see jarring transients, and the return leg revisits the same
landmarks so the matcher gets a second chance at every anchor

## 4.4 repeat phase

### 4.4.1 VIO + encoder fusion

`tf_wall_clock_relay_v55.py --slam-encoder` is the central fusion node.
~10-20 Hz.  it reads the current ORB-SLAM3 VIO pose, the integrated
wheel-encoder pose, optionally an anchor correction, and publishes
`map -> base_link`

Translation is SLAM-dominant - skid-steer encoders accumulate 0.5-1 %
per metre and wheel slip during in-place rotation makes it worse.  Yaw
is encoder + integrated gyro dominant, because ORB-SLAM3 yaw on a
mostly-forward platform is noisier than wheel odometry.  the four
regimes:

| regime | trigger | strategy |
|---|---|---|
| `no_anchor` | anchor age > 10 s | pure VIO + encoder, drifts but smooth |
| `ok` | fresh anchor, std >=0.15 | EMA fuse, alpha = 0.7 toward anchor |
| `strong` | >=2 fresh high-confidence anchors | higher weight toward anchor |
| `jump` | anchor disagrees by >3 m and tight covar | hard snap, no EMA |

`jump` is rare but it's the regime that saves long runs - if the
matcher has been silent and integrated drift outgrew the planner
tolerance, a discontinuous correction is the only thing that pulls
pose back.  `ok`'s EMA keeps small corrections smooth so the controller
does not see sudden jumps

Two gates filter spurious anchors.  The consistency gate
(`CONSISTENCY_M = 5 m`) drops anchors farther than that from the
current fused pose - probably wrong-landmark match.  The stale-anchor
reset falls back to `no_anchor` after 20 s of matcher silence so an
old correction does not keep dragging pose around

### 4.4.2 visual landmark matcher

`visual_landmark_matcher.py` runs at ~2 Hz.  per tick:

1. read current VIO pose, search teach `landmarks.pkl` for candidates
   within `CANDIDATE_RADIUS_M = 8 m` and within 90 deg of current
   heading.  keep top 5 by distance
2. extract ORB on the current RGB frame, BFMatcher cross-check against
   each candidate.  need >=10 geometric matches to keep
3. PnP-RANSAC: candidate's 3D keypoints (teach camera frame) vs current
   frame 2D keypoints.  inliers >=10 with median reprojection error
   <2 px required
4. compose anchor world pose, lift to base_link via the static
   base-to-camera offset
5. publish `/anchor_correction` as `PoseWithCovarianceStamped`,
   covariance diag inversely proportional to inlier count

Two early mistakes had to be undone.  First - a running landmark
accumulator (added new frames to the landmark set when matcher had been
silent for 5 s) created a drift reinforcement loop: a new landmark
got registered at a *drifted* pose, the next anchor latched onto it,
and drift grew unbounded.  Disabling it (`ACCUM_ENABLE = False`) cut
peak drift on landmark-sparse routes from 80+ m to the 2-10 m range.
Second - the consistency gate used to be 10 m, which sometimes
admitted wrong-building matches on repetitive corridors.  tightening
to 3-5 m killed those

### 4.4.3 goal dispatcher: WP look-ahead + detour ring

`send_goals_hybrid.py` is the route-level supervisor.  Instead of just
feeding teach waypoints into Nav2, it does three things:

- **start-WP selection**: scans the outbound half (`[0, n/2)`) of the
  teach WP list for the WP nearest the current robot pose.  needed
  because round-trip routes have spawn = last WP, and naive
  nearest-neighbour would lock onto the end.  also, for routes whose
  teach CSV starts a few metres from the Isaac spawn, a fast-skip
  counter (`MAX_PLAN_FAILS = 2` for the first `n/8` WPs) walks the
  sender forward until something is reachable
- **proactive WP projection**: subscribes to `/global_costmap/costmap`,
  on every update scans all teach WPs, and any WP whose cell costs
  more than `LETHAL_INFLATED = 70` is projected to the nearest free
  cell within 2 m.  if nothing free, WP gets flagged for skip
- **look-ahead detour ring**: before sending WP `i`, a check against
  known obstacle positions + the costmap cost decides if the WP is
  safe.  unsafe ones get replaced with a detour point on a 4-7 m ring
  around the obstacle.  detour point is itself tested against the
  costmap before dispatch.  once detour is reached, sender skips
  ahead instead of trying to return to the original blocked WP

REACH fires when `map -> base_link` is within `TOLERANCE = 3 m` of WP
xy.  same fused pose Nav2 plans against, not GT, so a small drift
cannot desync the planner from the reach test

### 4.4.4 local controller

`pure_pursuit_path_follower.py` reads `/plan` and writes `/cmd_vel`.
Lookahead is proportional to current linear velocity, clamped to
`[0.3, 2.5] m`.  Nav2's `controller_server` is not used at all -
regulated pure pursuit there runs its own local plan critics + speed
controller, which under VIO drift complicates things more than it
helps.  One controller on the published global plan is simpler and
predictable for evaluation

### 4.4.5 turnaround supervisor

`turnaround_supervisor.py` listens to the fused pose and fires when
the robot is within 10 m of the apex *and* has at some earlier sample
been more than 2 m past it.  The second condition distinguishes
actually crossed the apex from grazed near it on the outbound leg.
The signal writes `/tmp/isaac_remove_obstacles.txt`,
`run_husky_forest.py` polls and de-spawns the obstacle props for the
return leg.  this mirrors the real-world case where placed obstacles
are only there for the outbound run

## 4.5 evaluation

Three GT-based metrics, all post-hoc, computed by
`_common/scripts/compute_metrics.py`:

- **directional WP coverage**.  teach WPs are split at the WP closest
  to the turnaround; GT samples are split at the GT sample closest in
  time to it.  an outbound WP is visited only if at least one
  outbound GT sample lies within `R_TOL = 3 m` of it; a return WP
  likewise against the return GT segment.  this catches the "robot
  went out and never came back" mode that symmetric nearest-neighbour
  silently rewards
- **endpoint success**.  `reach_success` = min GT distance to apex
  <=10 m.  `return_success` = GT distance from final sample to spawn
  <=10 m, gated by a 50 % coverage floor so a robot that never moved
  cannot score
- **localization drift**.  every fusion-relay tick logs
  `err = |map->base_link - GT|`.  reported as mean / p95 / max.  the
  direct measurment of fusion quality and the main quantitative
  claim of the thesis

## 4.6 baselines

Two ablations share the teach artefacts and the scene:

- **stock Nav2 baseline (exp 74)**.  keeps Isaac, VIO, teach map, and
  the turnaround supervisor.  swaps `send_goals_hybrid` for stock
  `FollowWaypoints`, `pure_pursuit_path_follower` for Nav2's
  `controller_server` (regulated pure pursuit), and removes the
  matcher.  a WP-projection client still sits in front of
  `FollowWaypoints` for a fair chance against inflation.  isolates the
  value of anchor correction + route-level detour
- **RGB-D-only baseline (exp 76)**.  same proposed stack, except
  ORB-SLAM3 runs in pure RGB-D mode (no IMU branch).  everything else
  identical.  isolates the value of IMU fusion

Results are aggregated across 15 routes (150-400 m RT) of varying
geometry (corner-to-corner, corner-to-midpoint, interior cross) and
summarised in [`routes/README.md`](routes/README.md)

## 4.7 summary

The proposed solution is the minimum viable combination of six pieces:

- off-the-shelf RGB-D-Inertial VIO
- wheel encoders as a yaw-rate source + VIO fallback
- a visual landmark matcher giving periodic absolute anchor
  corrections against a teach database
- Nav2's global planner on a depth-derived static occupancy map
- a route-level goal supervisor (WP projection, look-ahead detours,
  round-trip turnaround)
- a simple pure-pursuit controller

No component is novel on its own.  The contribution is in the
integration plus the specific tunings (accumulator disabled,
consistency gate tightened to 3-5 m, WP projection + detour ring,
reach check on `map->base_link`) that let the stack survive long,
landmark-sparse, visually repetitive outdoor traversals
